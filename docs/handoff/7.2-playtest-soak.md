## 2. The two design rules that make it worth trusting

1. **The bot may only read what a real client can read.** It drives through boardgame.io's own client,
   so every state it sees has already been through `playerView` — no peeking into other hands, no
   reaching into `G.stack`. If the bot ever needs a fact the client isn't given, that is a finding
   about the client, not a licence to import the engine. (The one marked exception: it reads
   `content/standard/*.json` for card effectKeys — public data every client already ships.)
2. **It proposes; the server disposes.** The bot assembles *plausible* moves and detects an
   INVALID_MOVE by watching `_stateID` fail to advance. It does **not** re-derive the rules to
   pre-filter them — that would be writing a second engine, which is the thing engine-design §3 exists
   to prevent, and it would mean the harness could never disagree with the engine about what's legal.

## 3. What the soak asserts (every step of every game)

1. **Card conservation** — no card created or destroyed, across every zone. Catches a `moveCards` out
   of a zone the card wasn't in, or a reshuffle that dropped the discard pile.
2. **Engine/framework sync** — `ctx.currentPlayer` and `ctx.activePlayers` must agree with `G.pending`
   on *every* step, not just at turn boundaries. Drift here = the asked player cannot answer.
3. **No zombie** — the engine never returns to the turn loop (`act`/`discard`) with a living player at
   0 hp, which would mean a dying window was silently skipped. Asserted against the request KIND, not
   a bare hp check, because a living 0-hp player *is* correct between `damage.after` and the dying
   check (奸雄/反馈/刚烈/遗计 all resolve right there — see `game.test.ts`'s `TURN_LOOP_REQUESTS` note).
4. **No wedge** — every game terminates with a `gameOver` naming a legal side and ≥1 winner. Past the
   step cap, it fails and prints the seed, the general line-up, the pending request and the last 30
   requests.
5. **Every request is answerable** — the bot throws on a request kind it has never seen. That is the
   6.4b bug class (3.3 shipped `chooseCard` before the client could answer it, and the table stalled),
   now caught by a bot instead of by a player staring at a frozen board.

The soak also asserts it *actually played* — mean steps and turns per game must clear a floor, and at
least some Phase 4 skills must have fired. A harness that silently degrades into "every game ends on
step 3" would otherwise pass forever.

## 4b. Findings

### Finding #1 — the stale-`choices` wedge (FIXED). Found on the soak's first run.

**Symptom:** `bot: every move was refused for pending 'chooseCard' (player 1) — this is a WEDGE.` The
engine was blocked on a request that had **no legal answer at all**: a real table would hang forever,
and the player would be told their perfectly reasonable click was an invalid move, every time.

**Cause.** A multi-round discard (刚烈's two cards · 寒冰剑's two · 贯石斧's two) returns, from **one**
`resolve()` call, both the `{t:'moveCards'}` for the card just picked **and** the next
`{t:'request'}` asking for the next one. `resolve()` may never mutate `G` (engine-design §3), so it
necessarily builds that request's `choices` against a hand that **has not yet lost the card it is
about to lose**. By the time the request frame pops, the discard *has* applied — and the slots
describe a hand that no longer exists.

Usually that is one harmless dead slot at the end. It is **fatal when the victim holds exactly one
card**: round two offers slot 0, slot 0 now resolves to nothing, and every possible answer is
`INVALID_MOVE`.

**Fix — `engine/pump.ts`'s `'request'` case, not the three effects.** A `chooseCard`'s `choices` are
now re-validated against **live state at pop time**, exactly as U1 already does for an `act` request's
`legalTargets`, and the request is **skipped entirely when nothing is left to point at** (the resume
beneath then runs with no `chosen`, which all three effects already handle — "nothing left to take,
stop early, not an error"). The pusher cannot know what will be true at pop time, so the pusher must
not be the one to decide. Re-deriving also silently repairs the **index shift**: a hand's slots are
always the contiguous range `0..n-1`, so dropping the unresolvable ones leaves exactly the right set,
pointing at the right cards.

**Tests:** `server/test/engine/choiceStaleness.test.ts` — the wedge itself, the index shift, the
寒冰剑 twin, and the invariant stated once ("never leave a `chooseCard` pending whose choices cannot be
resolved").

**Why no unit test caught it, and the lesson.** 3.7's handoff (§3.2) *saw* this: two of its test files
originally asserted the discarded card would be gone from the second request's choices, found it still
there, and corrected the tests — reasoning, reasonably, that the live hand really does shrink by the
time the next request goes out. **That was right about the hand and wrong about the request**: the
frame is still carrying the slot list computed before the discard applied, and that list is what goes
out on the wire. A test that pins observed behaviour cannot tell you the observed behaviour is wrong.
Only playing the game can. This is the entire argument for the soak existing.

## 5. Known limits of this harness (say them out loud rather than imply coverage)

- **General selection (5.2) is not soaked.** The soak deals generals directly via `setupData`, so the
  `chooseGeneral` stage is never driven. It has its own tests (`bgio/selection.test.ts`); the bot names
  it explicitly in a `NOT_DRIVEN` set rather than skipping it silently.
- **No client, no UI.** Everything here is server-side. UX friction — the thing a human playtest is
  actually for — is untouched.
- **The bot plays badly on purpose.** It is a fuzzer, not an opponent: it accepts optional skills 75%
  of the time and picks targets at random. It is trying to *reach* strange states, not to win. Do not
  read anything into who wins.
- **A step-cap failure is not automatically a wedge.** If bots ever became too pacifist to kill each
  other, a game could run long and trip the cap. The failure message prints the turn count so the two
  are distinguishable; the bot is deliberately aggressive (it plays 杀/决斗/南蛮/万箭 first) to keep
  games ending.
